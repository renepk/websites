Biophysics Researcher 2009 – 2013

Johns Hopkins School of Medicine Baltimore, MD

Research in the laboratory of Dr. Jie Xiao focused on the division of e. coli bacteria, specifically on proteins FtsZ (filamentous temperature sensitive protein Z) and PBP3 (penicillin binding protein 3) and their biophysical attributes. Even though penicillin was one of the first antibiotics discovered, it has turned out to be one of the most effective and has avoided bacterial resistance for many decades. A very effective and clinically common class of antibiotics - the beta-lactem antibiotics - are direct descendants of penicillin. The most clinically widespread and insidious antibiotic resistant bacteria, namely MRSA (methicillin resistant staphylococcus aureus) is resistant to all derivatives of penicillin. My research was to push the imaging technology to a point where we could watch individual proteins acting in real time on this highly important division process. Cloning, microscopy and image analysis in Matlab were the main techniques used to try to elucidate the physical mechanism of protein action during the invagination and cleavage process.
